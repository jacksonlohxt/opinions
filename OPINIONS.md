## Durable Beliefs

### On Learning & CS Education
- **Practical coding beats theory-first.** The best preparation for CS is becoming familiar with the practical side — tools, debugging, and writing real code — not studying CS theory like relational database math or cryptography before you need it. You'll forget it anyway. [Note #128, 2026-06-28]
- **C is the right first language, not Python.** Harvard CS50x is the best intro CS course because it starts with C, which doesn't hold your hand and forces you to learn memory management early. Python as a first language hides pointers and memory, creating confusion later when concepts like pass-by-value vs pass-by-reference appear in other languages. [Note #128, 2026-06-28]
- **Learn debugging tools properly.** IDE debuggers (breakpoints, variable inspection) are better than print statements most of the time. If your course forces terminal-only exams, learn GDB — it's powerful but painful to use without the TUI layout. Valgrind is niche but essential for C memory debugging. [Note #128, 2026-06-28]
- **Vim is worth learning** if you'll face terminal-only programming exams. The keybinds are bizarre but efficient once internalized, and every major IDE has a vim keybinds plugin. [Note #128, 2026-06-28]
- **ChatGPT (GPT-4) is 100% worth paying for** in CS education — treat it as a personal tutor that explains things, not a source of unpaid labour. [Note #128, 2026-06-28]
- **NUS Security focus area isn't well taught.** Better off getting external certs like OSCP rather than relying on NUS security modules. [Note #128, 2026-06-28]

### On Writing & Communication
- **The Pyramid Principle is the most useful structure for writing.** Follow the structure and everything else follows. When you get lost while writing, return to the pyramid to know what you want to talk about. [Note #81, 2026-06-28]
- **Evidence must back every point.** Writing should not be solely from your own point of view — it needs evidence to support claims. Feedback confirmed this: an article written purely from POV without evidence is weak. [Note #81, 2026-06-28]
- **Be specific, not general.** Listing concrete examples and reflecting on real experiences makes writing stand out. It brings the reader to the place — they can visualize being there. [Note #101, 2026-06-28]
- **When making comparisons, use a synthesis matrix.** Side-by-side comparison structures make it easier for the reader to weigh differences. [Note #101, 2026-06-28]
- **Put yourself in the reader's shoes.** Think about what their concerns would be. Make things easy for them — hyperlinks, clear structure, showing effort in accessibility. It signals that you actually sat in their position. [Note #101, 2026-06-28]

### On Community & Belonging
- **Society lacks low-barrier spaces for adults to connect after age 25-30.** Friendships thin out, work dominates, and loneliness creeps in. People need community spaces beyond school and work. [Note #85, 2026-06-28]
- **A podcast channel can be more than content — it can be a platform for belonging.** Conversations that spark dialogue and build community give people the feeling of inclusion even when daily life grows isolating. A platform combining content creation with community connection could address the social inclusion gap for adults. [Notes #85, #89, 2026-06-29]
- **Existing community service structures exist but don't solve the belonging problem.** Donations, CCA initiatives, and volunteer activities provide service but not necessarily genuine social connection for isolated adults. [Note #91, 2026-06-29]

### On Human Psychology & Design
- **Introversion/extroversion can be reframed as conscious vs subconscious processing time.** Extroverts spend more time in the automatic/subconscious layer; introverts spend more time in the active conscious layer. This is detectable through body language — when someone shifts into conscious thinking mode, it's visible. Conscious processing is not weakness — it may be the higher-level thinking mode. [Note #6, 2026-06-29]
- **Every person operates with two selves:** (1) the auto-person (subconscious, automatic, the "lazy" one who gets rejected socially), and (2) the behind-the-scenes conscious thinker whose job is to make the auto-person's outputs socially acceptable and useful. Good tools serve the conscious thinker by offloading organizational overhead. [Note #6, 2026-06-29]
- **The core bottleneck in thinking is not idea generation — it's organization.** If you can reduce the energy spent on categorizing and structuring, the conscious mind can focus fully on creating. This is the design thesis behind a second brain tool. [Note #6, 2026-06-29]

### On Technology & Human Nature
- **Technology should adapt to how humans naturally think, not the other way around.** Rather than humans learning to communicate with computers (learning coding languages), we should remove that step — let humans type in natural language and have the system understand them at the human layer. [Note #8, 2026-06-28]
- **AI helps separate two dimensions of being human:** (1) the human part — compassion, tolerance for pain, determination after setbacks; (2) the intelligence part — pattern recognition, computation, analysis. AI can handle the second, leaving humans to focus on the first. [Note #11, 2026-06-28]
- **MOBA games (like Dota 2) and coding are fundamentally the same activity.** Both give you a set of resources and ask: how do you max-min those resources to achieve the best result? The strategic thinking transfers. [Note #11, 2026-06-28]
- **People need a "second brain" that deeply understands them.** The best product is one that is closely related to the user — it understands their context without judgment and creates clarity from their mental chaos. Apple keeps things simple to remove friction between user and product; a second brain should do the same for thinking. [Note #8, 2026-06-28]

### On Product & Entrepreneurship
- **Great startups apply already accepted societal behaviors to underserved domains.** We normalize online-first commerce and trust everywhere — except in domains like amateur sports coaching, which remain hyper-local and fragmented by luck. [Note #15, 2026-06-28]

## Recurring Themes

- **The Dota-to-life metaphor.** Across multiple notes, MOBA games appear as a lens for understanding life: roles (pos 1-5), resource optimization, formation, covering each other's weaknesses, the "dark forest" of uncertainty. This isn't just a gaming reference — it's a recurring framework for thinking about teams, strategy, and individual roles. [Notes #8, #11, 2026-06-28]

- **Human layer vs machine layer.** A recurring two-part model: there is the human dimension (connection, emotion, experience, compassion) and the machine/intelligence dimension (computation, efficiency, AI). The goal is not to merge them but to let each do what it does best, with technology serving the human side. [Notes #8, #11, 2026-06-28]

- **Reducing friction.** Whether in product design (second brain app), community building (low-barrier social spaces), or learning (natural language over code), the recurring instinct is to remove the step between intention and action. [Notes #5, #8, #85, 2026-06-28]

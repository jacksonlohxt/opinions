## Durable Beliefs

- **The Pyramid Principle is the most useful structure for writing.** Follow the structure and everything else follows. When you get lost while writing, return to the pyramid to know what you want to talk about. [Note #81, 2026-06-28]
- **Evidence must back every point.** Writing should not be solely from your own point of view — it needs evidence to support claims. An article written purely from POV without evidence is weak. [Note #81, 2026-06-28]
- **Be specific, not general.** Listing concrete examples and reflecting on real experiences makes writing stand out. It brings the reader to the place — they can visualize being there. [Note #101, 2026-06-28]
- **When making comparisons, use a synthesis matrix.** Side-by-side comparison structures make it easier for the reader to weigh differences. [Note #101, 2026-06-28]
- **Put yourself in the reader's shoes.** Think about what their concerns would be. Make things easy for them — hyperlinks, clear structure, showing effort in accessibility. It signals that you actually sat in their position. [Note #101, 2026-06-28]
- **Good analysis examines solutions from both sides.** Present why some approaches are ineffective alongside why the proposed solution works. Arguments backed by evidence from both angles are more persuasive than one-sided advocacy. [Note #76, 2026-06-29]
- **Communication is flow, not memorization.** Effective communication comes from natural expression and compressing energy into single moments — not from pre-planned scripts. Taking breaks and admitting "I can't continue this topic" is normal and okay. Energy for conversation is finite and cycles between output and rest. [Note #133, 2026-06-29]
